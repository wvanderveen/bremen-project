The procedure of age estimations

For estimating migrants' ages, the youth welfare services first interview them in a so called “visual inspection” (“qualifizierte Inaugenscheinnahme”). If there are doubts or if the migrants request it, a medical age estimation is carried out. There, forensic doctors take X-rays, MRI or CT scans of people's teeth, collarbones and hands. How do migrants experience and analyze this?

Age estimation at the Youth Welfare Offices

“Then they said: ‘You are lying to us, that is not your age, we are giving you our own age.’”
Read full quote
“Yeah, I would say the age process in Bremen is really crazy, because the first time I came here I went to the Jugendamt where they take the minors. Then, I was registered there, I went through this process that you have to go to the police and there they take fingerprints. And a few days later, you have to go to the Sozialamt where they interview you and ask you to explain your journey to Europe. They take pictures of you also, from your side-face. Then, they ask you questions: ‘How old are you?’ and you give your age and when you were born and how your journey was, as well, coming to Europe. Many times, these informations are rejected, because what they told me was that I was lying. By looking at me — as a Black person — I feel it was racism because I was totally rejected just by looking at my body, looking at me, that I don't look young. Then they said to me, ‘You are lying to us, that's not your age. In fact, we are giving you our own age.’ So they have to give you the age that they think you are. They told me, ‘This is how old you are,’ and there is no way you can defend it. I had a lawyer, and he went to appeal the case and at the end of the day, it did not work. This is how it was.”

“How did they know? Are they my fathers or are they my mothers?.... They just come from nowhere and say: ‘This is your age!’”
Read full quote
“...When I came to Germany, I went to Bundes. When I got to Bundes, they took all my informations about me, myself — I gave all my information to them. So I was there, like, for three weeks. (...) And then they took me to the doctors and to the police to take my fingerprint and everything. I stayed there for three weeks and then I went to the interview with Social about the age estimation and so on.” Q: “What was the Social interview about? Was it about age or was it about your whole process of staying? Was it about asylum, or—?” A: “I don't even know what it was for them. They just call you and actually, I don't think they ask you about your age. They just said they are psychologists so they know exactly your age just by looking at you. But they don't ask anything about your age. They just ask you questions: ‘When were you born? How did you come to Europe? How did you came here in Germany?’ They ask you about all sorts of those things and also take pictures of you. They do all their stuff, but I don't remember if they ask questions like, ‘How old are you?’ But, of course, they asked, ‘When were you born?’ and you give them the answer. So that's what they do, I guess. If I can remember because it's been a long time. So I think I forgot how they did it exactly.” Q: “After this whole interview, what is the outcome of it?” A: “The outcome is, for example: If you give your age to them, and you said, for example, ‘I am 17 or 16 or 15,’ they will just make up their own mind and say ‘You are this or that age.’ So that's the outcome. They will just make their own decision about your age and say, ‘We don't believe the age you gave to us, so this is the age you have.’ So I don't know how they know what my age is, whether they are my father or my mother — I don't even know. They just come from nowhere and say: ‘This is your age. You have no proof.’”

“They made me older than my big sister!”
Read full quote
“...This was not easy for me to handle at that very time because I have really felt alone. This was a bit crazy for me. The thing was like, my hands looked older than my age. So this is crazy, you know? (laughs gently) It is really really crazy, you know. Yeah, they said, sometimes, I don't talk like a kid or my voice is too heavy. This is very very crazy and funny too, you know. (...) The worst part of this was, they didn't accept my age but they have to give me an age. They gave to 10 people — more than 30 people of the boys — like we all have the same age. So this is very very crazy, you know. And like this age was like very very big. The young age at that time [was] 24. At that time, my big sister from same mother was like 23.” Q: “They made you older than your sister?” A: “Yeah, it was really really funny. I even have to tell my sister about it, just how funny and stupid — we have to cope with it. We have no choice, it is how the system goes here.” Q: “Yeah, you only have to cope, yeah. Okay, like you claimed to be 16 oder?” A: “No, I was 17.” Q: “17. And now they give you 24? So like, how did this felt for you? Somebody you don't know interviewing you and asking you stories and everything and at the end saying, ‘I don't trust you. You are this age and you have to accept it. You have to take it.’” A: “At that very point, because, before going for that interview, someone told me that if they didn't accept your age, they normally gave you another age. So at that time, I realized that they told me that you have hands like not that of an underage and your voice also is too deep. And then I asked the guy like, ‘What do you think now is my age?’ The guy told me that he didn't know.  Like, they will have to send me a letter. They gave me a letter from that interview and asked me to take it to my camp and show it to my social workers. There, it was written my age, the age that they fixed for me, like this fixed one, you know? In that letter, I realized that I have to go to another place to fight for the age, fight the decision they made. This was a long process.”

"Teeth and Truth"  | Ethnographic analyses of medical age estimation

Teeth and Truth? Age IDentities of Migrants in the Making

Through a study of the technologies of assessment, this paper shows that the making of age IDentity is relational and ambiguous, and not a truth that is fixed in one's body as ‘biological age’. This challenges one of the promises of biometrics, an ability to measure ‘the body’ objectively. Rather, the paper proposes that what that body precisely is is an outcome of a specific practice.

→ Read more in Ethnos

Enacting biorelational citizenship in X-ray rooms and offices - on age assessments of migrants in Germany

This ethnographic analysis of forensic age estimations in Hamburg, Germany, shows that one cannot simply read age from a young migrant's bones. Rather, an individual's age is enacted in a practice whereby age is not only dependent on a body, but also on the specific bureaucratic and scientific traditions, technologies and standards involved in assessing it.
